## Origins

Operations Research grew from the use of scientific methods to improve operational decisions. Early roots include probability, statistics, industrial engineering, teletraffic analysis, logistics, economics, and mathematical optimization.

## World War II and Military OR

During World War II, interdisciplinary teams of scientists, engineers, and mathematicians were asked to improve radar deployment, convoy routing, anti-submarine warfare, aircraft utilization, bombing strategy, and logistics. The term Operations Research became associated with applying quantitative analysis to operational military decisions.

## Linear Programming and the Simplex Method

George Dantzig developed the simplex method in the late 1940s while working on planning problems for the U.S. Air Force. Linear programming became one of the central tools of OR because it provided a general language for resource allocation under constraints.

## Dynamic Programming

Richard Bellman developed dynamic programming in the 1950s for sequential decision problems. Dynamic programming later became foundational for Markov decision processes, optimal control, reinforcement learning, inventory control, and many stochastic decision models.

## Queueing Theory

A. K. Erlang's early 20th-century work on telephone systems created the mathematical foundation for queueing theory. Queueing models became central to telecommunications, service systems, healthcare operations, call centers, computing systems, and traffic analysis.

## Inventory Theory and Operations Management

Inventory theory emerged from production, logistics, and retail decisions: how much to order, when to order, how to balance service and cost, and how to cope with uncertainty. The EOQ model, newsvendor model, base-stock policies, and multi-echelon systems became core OR tools.

## Computing and Mathematical Programming

As digital computers matured, OR shifted from hand-solved models to computational optimization. Branch-and-bound, cutting planes, decomposition, interior-point methods, simulation languages, and specialized solvers made large industrial models practical.

## Modern Analytics

Modern OR overlaps heavily with analytics, data science, machine learning, and software engineering. Practitioners combine prediction, optimization, simulation, experiments, visualization, and deployment to support recurring operational decisions.

## AI and OR Convergence

AI and OR now intersect through reinforcement learning, decision-focused learning, ML-assisted branch-and-bound, learned heuristics, digital twins, planning, constraint programming, and prescriptive analytics. OR contributes rigor, constraints, robustness, and decision quality; AI contributes prediction, representation learning, and adaptive policies.

## Timeline

| Period | Milestone |
|---|---|
| 1909-1917 | Erlang develops mathematical models for telephone traffic and congestion. |
| 1930s | Scientific management, industrial engineering, statistics, and logistics mature as precursors to OR. |
| 1939-1945 | World War II accelerates military Operations Research in the UK and US. |
| 1944 | von Neumann and Morgenstern publish foundational work in game theory. |
| 1947 | Dantzig develops the simplex method for linear programming. |
| 1950s | Dynamic programming, inventory theory, queueing theory, and simulation expand rapidly. |
| 1957 | INFORMS predecessor societies and OR professional communities grow in the US and UK. |
| 1960s | Integer programming, branch-and-bound, network flows, and scheduling theory advance. |
| 1970s | Complexity theory reshapes understanding of combinatorial optimization. |
| 1980s | Interior-point methods transform linear and convex optimization. |
| 1990s | Commercial solvers, algebraic modeling languages, and enterprise OR systems expand. |
| 2000s | Large-scale MIP, stochastic programming, robust optimization, and simulation optimization mature. |
| 2010s | Open-source solvers, Python/Julia modeling, analytics, and cloud deployment broaden access. |
| 2020s | OR increasingly converges with AI, reinforcement learning, digital twins, and decision intelligence. |
